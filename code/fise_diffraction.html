Comparing two sources of blur: Pinholes and Diffraction

We can summarize image system blur by measuring how light from a point source is spread on the image. The image of a point is called the point spread function. We will see many point spread functions in this book.
For a pinhole camera, two factors contribute to the blur: the pinhole diameter and the diffraction.
The larger the pinhole the larger the blur. Consider a distant point, so that the rays arrive at the pinhole and they are parallel. As the diameter of the pinhole diameter increases, the rays will be spread over an area that roughly matches the pinhole size.
Second, consider the light from a distant point as a wave. Then as the pinhole diameter decreases, the rays will spread over a larger angle.
As the pinhole diameter increases, the blur from these two different physical factors have opposite effects. When the pinhole is large the blur is mainly from the pinhole, and when the pinhole is small the blur is mainly from diffraction.
This script calculates which effect dominates for different pinhole sizes.

Choosing parameters

Let's explore for this range of diameters, from about 100 microns to 1200 microns (1.2mm). The wavelength of the light will matter also for the wave calculation. Let's pick a short wavelength.
pinholeD = (100:100:2000)*1e-6; % Pinhole diameter (meters)
wave = 400e-9; % Wavelength (meters)
As Lord Airy described in 1835, we can calculate the expected image as waves pass through a circular pinhole. We calculate these in ISETCam using this code.
fNumber = 8; % Longer wavelength
[~,img] = airyDisk(wave,fNumber,'units','um');
*** The oi fov is not set yet. Using a default fov of 10. oiGet: No scene, arbitrary oi angle: 10 deg
ieNewGraphWin;
mesh(img.x,img.y,img.data);
xlabel('Position (um)'); ylabel('Position (um)'); axis square
Notice that there is a big central peak (the Airy disk) and then a series of small rings, separated by zero-crossings. If we look at the image, the surrounding rings are very dim.
ieNewGraphWin;
imagesc(img.x,img.y,img.data);
axis image; colormap(0.7*gray+0.3); xlabel('Position (um)'); ylabel('Position (um)');

Airy Disk size

The size of the central region is called the Airy Disk. The diameter of the disk has a simple formula that we can express as the angle swept out by the the light emerging from the pinhole. The angle is and the distance to the wall is L. If we know those values, we can calculate the size of the spread on the wall.
This is the formula for the Airy Disk radius in degrees
diffractionDeg = asind(1.22*(wave ./ pinholeD));
 
% L meters away
L = 1;
 
From standard geometry, here is the relationship between the angle and the radius
% Let's make the angle in degrees, and thus use tand(), and we
% multiply by 2 to get the diffraction diameter
diffractionD = 2*L*tand(diffractionDeg);
This graph plots the spread from diffraction and from the pinhole itself, as a function of pinhole diameter. The two lines intersect at the pinhole size where the two effects are equal. When the wall is 1 meter away, the effects are equal for a pinhole diameter a little less than 1 mm.
ieNewGraphWin;
unit = 'mm';
loglog(pinholeD*ieUnitScaleFactor(unit),diffractionD*ieUnitScaleFactor(unit),'-');
hold on;
loglog([0;pinholeD(:)]*ieUnitScaleFactor(unit),[0;pinholeD(:)]*ieUnitScaleFactor(unit),'k--');
xlabel(sprintf('Pinhole diameter (%s)',unit));
ylabel(sprintf('Blur diameter (%s)',unit));
grid on; legend({'Diffraction','Pinhole'});
Below this pinhole size, diffraction dominates. Larger than this size, pinhole blur dominates.

How does this depend on the distance to the wall?

ieNewGraphWin;
unit = 'mm';
 
% Distances are 5, 50 and 500 millimeters
pinholeD = (10:10:1200)*1e-6; % Pinhole diameter (meters)
diffractionDeg = asind(1.22*(wave ./ pinholeD));
 
Lmeters = [0.001, 0.01, 0.1]; % Meters
match = zeros(size(Lmeters));
 
loglog([0;pinholeD(:)]*ieUnitScaleFactor(unit),[0;pinholeD(:)]*ieUnitScaleFactor(unit),'k--');
hold on;
for ii=1:numel(Lmeters)
% Radius size on a wall, L meters away
L = Lmeters(ii);
diffractionD = 2*L*tand(diffractionDeg);
p = loglog(pinholeD*ieUnitScaleFactor(unit),diffractionD*ieUnitScaleFactor(unit),'-','LineWidth',2);
%{
% If you want the exact match points
[v,idx] = min(abs(pinholeD - diffractionD));
match(ii) = pinholeD(idx)*ieUnitScaleFactor(unit);
fprintf('L = %f (m): Equal blur %f (%s)\n',Lmeters(ii),match(ii),unit);
%}
end
xlabel(sprintf('Pinhole diameter (%s)',unit));
ylabel(sprintf('Blur diameter (%s)',unit)); grid on
legend({'pinhole','0.001','0.01','0.1'})
As the wall gets closer to the pinhole, the diffraction blur shrinks. The graph is quite simple when the axes are logarithmic.
